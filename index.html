How This Paper Was Born — The Real Story
Gabriella Kasdaglis was working hard to memorize as many digits of π as possible for her school challenge. Her dad — Dr. Nicholas Kasdaglis — saw the moment and asked a question: "If science tells us π is infinite, does that mean the discussion is over? Or can we still question science?" Matthew, her twin, was there listening.

The lesson: science is not an authority to obey — it is a method for exploring. It doesn't draw final lines. It shows us where the next questions live. That conversation became this paper and its title: Finitum Non Capax Infiniti — the finite is not capable of the infinite.

Finitum Non Capax Infiniti — The Deeper Principle
This Latin phrase — "the finite is not capable of the infinite" — is 500 years old. Lutheran theologians used it to describe how physical creation cannot contain the fullness of the infinite. Dr. Kasdaglis showed it is also a law of physics: no bounded region of space can instantiate infinite mathematical precision. The math is the Kasdaglis Limit. The truth it describes is the Finitum Principle — and it belongs to the order of things, not to any one person.
💡 The Big Idea
In math class, π goes on forever: 3.14159265358979… But we live in a physical universe with finite space and energy. Any region of space can only hold a limited amount of information (the Bekenstein Bound — real, established physics). So the universe can only ever use a finite number of digits of π. The rest exist in pure mathematics — but in physical reality they have no address to live at.

Important: π does not stop being irrational or infinite. The mathematics is unchanged. The claim is that physical observables cannot distinguish contributions below the entropy-limited resolution.

S ≤ (π · R²) / (4 · lP²)

🎲 State Count & the Two-Tier Framework
N ≈ R²/lP²  (2D screen)  ·  n ≈ √N ≈ R/lP (1D fiber)
N = states on the 2D boundary (sets area precision ΔΣ = lP²) · n = states on the 1D loop (sets Cn group)
